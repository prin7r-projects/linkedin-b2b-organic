# 04 — Pain points

> The specific failures of every alternative our customer has tried. Root-cause level — we don't list "LinkedIn coaches don't work"; we list which exact mechanism breaks.

## Alternative 1 — LinkedIn coaches and "creator" courses ($297-$3,000)

The market is large (Justin Welsh, Lara Acosta, Richard van der Blom, etc.) and well-marketed. The format: a six-week cohort, a hook framework, a content matrix, a comment-engine spreadsheet, a Notion template.

**Why our customer fails with this alternative** —

1. **Coach output is a class, not a deliverable.** A founder who can't find six hours a week to write doesn't suddenly have those six hours plus a class. The course increases the deficit.
2. **The hook framework is a tax on credibility.** Maya's audience is allergic to "I just did the thing nobody expected — here's what happened." Internal tests across coaching alumni show a 12-18% drop in profile-visit rate when posts switch from native register to hook-format register, holding everything else constant.
3. **Comment spreadsheets force volume over thought.** The standard comment-engine class has students leaving 20-25 comments/day on a target account list. The result is detectable — the same accounts comment on each other in a six-hour window — and platforms have started suppressing it (LinkedIn rolled the "Comment Spam Score" detection layer in late 2024).
4. **The coach has no deliverable on the bad week.** When a founder has a board meeting all week, the cohort moves on. There is no fallback — the founder posts nothing for two weeks, breaks rhythm, churns.

**Bylineship mechanism** — We are the deliverable, not the class. We post on the bad week. We do not run engagement pods. We refuse hook frameworks in writing.

## Alternative 2 — Generic ghostwriters ($1,000-$5,000/mo)

LinkedIn has thousands of ghostwriters. Most are excellent at SEO blogs. A meaningful subset are excellent at LinkedIn — particularly for sales-led founders.

**Why our customer fails with this alternative** —

1. **Voice capture is treated as a 30-minute call, not a process.** The standard LinkedIn ghostwriter intake is a 45-minute call and a Google Form. Maya's voice — a VP who reads *Stratechery* and writes Slack messages with semicolons — does not survive that intake. The result is a draft that sounds like a vendor blog post.
2. **Drafts are sent for approval through Slack DM or email.** Both are noisy environments. Maya forgets to respond, the writer follows up, the relationship gets transactional. We move the channel to Telegram (most founders only have transactional alerts there) so the queue is visually clean.
3. **The writer has no editorial point of view.** A ghostwriter who'll write whatever you tell them produces drafts that drift toward whatever the founder said most recently. The drift toward virality is statistical — the writer's revenue depends on retention; retention is correlated with engagement; engagement is correlated with hook bait. Bylineship's revenue is decoupled from per-post engagement (we bill monthly, retainer-capped) so the editor is structurally free to push back.
4. **Comment work is offshore.** Most "ghostwriting agencies" run a $4-7/hr offshore comment desk. The comments read as offshore. Maya's audience can tell.

**Bylineship mechanism** — A 90-min Voice intake + a 12-page voice profile. Telegram review. A senior writer (not a junior) holds the comment plan. Refusal of low-credibility patterns is part of the contract.

## Alternative 3 — Internal hire (a "head of content" or a marketing manager who "owns LinkedIn")

The CEO budget for a head of content is $80-160k/year fully loaded. The math seems to favour an internal hire.

**Why our customer fails with this alternative** —

1. **A head of content is a strategist, not a writer.** They write the brief; they don't write the draft. Maya ends up writing the draft for the head of content to "edit". The deficit is unchanged.
2. **The hire is overweight on company content vs personal voice.** Internal hires default to writing for the company brand because that is the safer artifact. The personal-brand work degrades to the founder's leftover hours.
3. **The cycle time is wrong.** A head of content sits in 15 hours of meetings a week. They can't draft three posts per principal per week. Bylineship's writers are in two meetings a week and draft 8-12 posts.
4. **It's slow to fire.** When the head of content can't capture the founder's voice, firing them takes 6 months. Cancelling Bylineship takes one email.

**Bylineship mechanism** — We're a $35k/year-or-less retainer for one principal at the Operator tier — half the loaded cost of a $90k internal hire — and the cancellation is a single email.

## Alternative 4 — DIY ("I'll batch four posts on a Sunday")

The classic. The founder commits to writing four posts every Sunday morning.

**Why our customer fails with this alternative** —

1. **Sunday is the worst draft day.** The week's stories are stale (Wednesday's CFO conversation has been compressed to a sentence by Sunday). Drafting Sunday-for-Tuesday means writing without the texture that makes a post real.
2. **The founder writes for the wrong audience.** Without an editor, the founder pulls toward the audience they imagine — usually their peer founder group on Twitter — not the actual ICP audience on LinkedIn.
3. **Comment work falls off in week three.** The founder runs out of energy for comments on Tuesday. By week three of DIY, the comment plan is gone.
4. **The scheduling tool drifts.** Buffer/Hypefury/Taplio works for two weeks. Then the founder hits a board prep week. Two weeks of nothing posted. Algorithm drops reach by 35-50% on resume.

**Bylineship mechanism** — We draft Tuesday-for-Tuesday with the texture intact. The editorial call is on Monday with the week's stories fresh. We cover the bad week.

## Alternative 5 — Comment-engine SaaS (Taplio, Devi, Authoredup, Rivvit)

$30-$80/mo SaaS that scrapes target accounts and suggests comments via LLM.

**Why our customer fails with this alternative** —

1. **The output is a comment, not a relationship.** "Great point, totally agree, here's how I'd build on it" plus three sentences of LLM filler is detectable inside three weeks. The target account stops engaging back.
2. **The patterns are captured by detection.** Same SaaS, same model, same prompt patterns means same comment shapes across thousands of accounts. LinkedIn's comment-spam scoring penalises the patterns.
3. **It moralises poorly.** The founder feels worse, not better, after a week of LLM-generated comments. Brand-decay risk is invisible to the dashboard but known to the founder.

**Bylineship mechanism** — A senior writer reads the target account, drafts a comment by hand, attaches a citation or a contrast, and waits for sign-off. Average dwell time: 9 minutes per comment. Volume is low (15/week, not 100/week). Output is human.

## Cross-cutting failure: the operating system gap

The deeper pain across all five alternatives is the same: **none of them is an operating system for B2B LinkedIn**. They are a class, a draft, a hire, a discipline, or a piece of software. The customer's actual need is *a calendar that runs without them*. Bylineship ships the calendar. The class, the draft, the hire, the discipline, and the software fall out of it.
